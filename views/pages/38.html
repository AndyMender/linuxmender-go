Summer is over and Fall is just 'round the corner. The time of harvest is nigh, with its pumpkin-headed harbinger in its vanguard. For me the season of orange-brown kaleidoscopes and withering leaves is always a time of recollection and deep melancholy. However, still holding onto the sunny mood, like the last warm gust, I decided to devote this entry to a round-up of several Linux distributions I found most worthwhile for both greenhorns and veterans alike. There might be numbers, but they mean completely nothing.

  • Debian

    The father, mother, (sister? daughter?) and grandparent of most Linux distributions. The oldest in this round-up. Debian can be literally anything. It can be an ultra-stable, unbreakable server (Debian Stable), an all-around desktop operating system (Debian Testing) or a developer playground (Debian Unstable). There are very few things which Debian does not do well. Unfortunately for me, one of them is dependency resolution. In its attempt to be helpful, Debian sometimes links too many packages in a web of dependencies, causing artificial conflicts. This happens very rarely, though, and is swiftly resolved by package maintainers.

  • The Ubuntu family

    The descendants of Debian. A group of distributions created to show people that Linux can be attractive, fully functional and most importantly competitive as compared to Windows and MacOS X. Also, that it is most likely the operating system model of the future. Thanks to its many flavors, Ubuntu is suited for most hardware, even rather dated, like 32-bit PowerPC.

  • Arch Linux

    The Linux of the people. Debian gives choice, Arch Linux means choice. It is the very essence of Linux (with a package manager!). It gives the user a set of basic tools to create his/her perfect operating system and thorough guidelines explaining how to do it. There are completely no limits to how far one go with it.

  • Manjaro

    The first-born of Arch. If Arch Linux seems too daunting, there is Manjaro. Many aspects of Arch Linux have been optimized and streamlined to provide top-notch out-of-the-box experience. There are kinks and rough edges sometimes, however this should come as no surprise, since it’s very difficult to build on something as do-it-yourself as Arch Linux.

  • Fedora

    The grand innovator. Driven entirely by its vast community of dedicated users, designers and developers. It’s more streamlined than Arch and has its base in the legendary Red Hat. Truly, while Arch is geared towards tinkerers, Fedora seems to be the distribution of serious creators and software developers. Again, sky is the limit...but it’s always blue.
