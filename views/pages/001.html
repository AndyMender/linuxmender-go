As I mentioned in my last entry, I decided to embark on a journey to the mythical lands of Linux, because I needed something in my computing life to change. I was overly annoyed with the bloat-ish nature of Windows 7 and was quite unwilling to try either Windows 8/8.1 or Windows 10 in the future. Mac OS X was out of the question for obvious reasons – the price. Linux seemed like a good, inexpensive alternative. It also potentially offered what I was looking for – lightness and flexibility.

When I was browsing the Internet I encountered an immediate roadblock in my path to convertion. Linux is not a single operating system. Now, laugh all you want, but when it comes to technology and geeky activities I really struggle to make a decision...

  • Programming in Python or Perl? Or maybe another language-of-the-month?
  • Role-playing as a healing priest or as a damage-dealing warlock?
  • My magical staff should give off a blue or perhaps a green aura?

    • The list goes on and on. In MMORPGs I typically spend more time in the character creation screen than in the tutorial area. And suddenly I’m forced to choose among hundreds of Linux flavors (distributions). Completely confounded I decided (ha!) to limit my choices to so-called lightweight distributions. Although not a category per se, many development teams strive to offer fully functional Linux variants for people using both old and new hardware. After a few hours of searching and reading I drafted a list of potentially worthwhile lightweight Linux distributions:

  • Crunchbang (Waldorf 11)

    A relatively simple distribution based on Debian (the predecessor of the popular Ubuntu). It doesn’t really use a desktop environment, unlike Windows or a lot of other Linux distributions. Instead, it embraces the incredible flexibility of the Openbox window manager. The desktop can be freely adjusted to one's needs. As Crunchbang is based on Debian, it is very stable. Unfortunately, it also requires basic knowledge of Linux commands, which I did not have. Probably not the best choice for a greenhorn like me.

  • Lubuntu (14.04 LTS or 14.10)

    A variant of Ubuntu designed for older computers. The LXDE (Lightweight X11 Desktop Environment) desktop environment looked simple, yet quite attractive. It had a feel very similar to Windows. However, Windows was something I wanted to avoid at all costs at that point. Nevertheless, as it is a branch of Ubuntu, it is bound to have great community and development team support, including regular updates. Thus, I decided to leave it for later.

  • Peppermint Five (14.04 LTS)

    A Linux distribution based on Lubuntu. Just like Lubuntu it utilizes LXDE, however with added eyecandy. Peppermint is often advertised as an internet-centric distribution, meaning that most applications are related to Internet activities or rely on browser-based components (like Adobe Flash Player). Although, the developers claimed that one can use Peppermint locally as well. Later perhaps?

  • Bodhi Linux (2.4.0 or 3.0.0 Release Candidate 1

    Based on the current Ubuntu LTS (2.4.0 relies on Ubuntu 12.04 LTS, while 3.0.0 on Ubuntu 14.04 LTS), Bodhi strives for lightness. It was clearly designed as THE lightweight distribution. It also had that underdog feel to it – like a rebel among other proper Linux distributions. Interestingly, unlike Crunchbang, Bodhi didn’t sacrifice visuals for lightness. Bodhi Linux uses the unique Enlightenment (E) desktop environment. E17 shipped with Bodhi Linux 2.4.0 offers a number of themes to choose from initially, while additional ones can be found online.

    • The moment I saw the beautiful Enlightenment desktop and read Bodhi’s Path to Enlightenment guide I knew what my decision would be...
