Since my very early childhood I always enjoyed tinkering with electronics. I used to disassemble toy trains, cars and motorcycles. After I had received my first PC, this habit escalated and items such as joysticks and gaming pads fell victim to my tinkering urges. As time went by, my targets increased in size and I began fiddling with desktop PCs. I truly enjoyed altering and improving everything around me. This included software. Sadly, Windows as a tinkering platform isn't particularly malleable.

First off, software for Windows is usually available only in binary form and protected by numerous copyright laws. Therefore, even if you somehow manage to decompile it or understand the configuration files well enough to hack it, the capacity to publish your findings is very limited. When you acquire a piece of software, you actually own only the license to use it. Same as with books - you are entitled only to the single paper or electronic copy you purchased.

Sadly, this copyright-entrenched distribution model is extremely restrictive and doesn't allow the vast community of developers to profit from the software's inception. Also, this distribution model stands in stark contrast to open-source and free software, which usually can be freely distributed, modified and further redistributed. In my opinion, the freedom to tinker with any piece of software is extremely beneficial to its longevity, but also augments the overall quality of software with a similar purpose. Study of software freedom eventually led me to the work of Richard Matthew Stallman.

Richard Matthew Stallman (nicknamed RMS on Internet forums) is a freedom activist, Linux developer and programmer. The list of his achievements is so long that one could easily write a book (or two!) to try to cover his life. I personally hold RMS in very high regard and consider his Four Freedoms to be of equal importance to Isaac Asimov’s Three Laws of Robotics. However, when discussing software freedom, one should consider the possible extremes – lack of freedom (tyranny) and overabundance of freedom (anarchy). Neither extreme is particularly favorable, of course.

To briefly recapitulate Stallman’s Four Freedoms:

  • 1. The freedom to run the program as you wish, for any purpose.
  • 2. The freedom to study the program and to modify it according to your needs.
  • 3. The freedom to distribute the original software to help others.
  • 4. The freedom to redistribute your modifications so that everyone can profit from them.

Points 2. 3. and 4. assume access to the program's source code. However, a piece of software has to fulfill all points to be considered truly free. While I agree with points 1. and 2., I'm somewhat on the fence with points 3. and 4.

Points 1. and 2. make sense to me, because the usual involvement of money in software distribution according to the commercial model taints the relationship between users and developers. The user suddenly becomes a regular customer, with expectations proportional to the money spent on the product. The relationship is made even more unhealthy by the fact that often the user has access to the software only after concluding the purchase. Therefore, he or she actually pays for the promise, rather than for the product. Back in the day we used to have demos, trial versions and shareware editions of software. While one could assume these were used to entice the prospective customer, at least he or she was allowed to sample the product before committing.

Now, this view of mine contradicts with my opinion on points 3. and 4. At least partially. It would of course be great if all software out there was free to use and modify, but that leaves a lot less room for business success stories. More so, it would force companies to always develop software from scratch, each their own. This is often the case in the games industry, though it makes little sense in many other fields. That is why software of potential commercial importance is typically released under a more permissive license, such as MIT, Apache2 or BSD.

To conclude, I believe Stallman’s Four Freedoms are of great import and should be applied to software when possible. However, it is also acceptable for companies to run businesses on open-source software, because it showcases the usefulness of certain programs and indirectly incentivizes companies to contribute back.
