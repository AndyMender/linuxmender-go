I first came in contact with open-source communities when developing modifications (mods) for the popular Bethesda Softworks gaming title The Elder Scrolls: Skyrim. While the game itself is proprietary, Bethesda released a number of tools that made it possible for players to expand the game and freely share their creations. Producing various pieces of in-game apparel I quickly began to understand how a good community should function. The basics were rather simple â€“ sharing and caring.

To me everything was simply a matter of socialist economy applied on a very small scale. Let us consider a trivial example:

  • There are 10 craftsmen, each specializing in a different craft.
  • Each produces a single type of goods, but thanks to common distribution channels (the Internet), he/she can freely share them with all the other craftsmen.
  • As a result, each craftsman profits 10-fold by contributing with his/her own work and in return gaining access to products outside of his / her area of expertise (SHARING).
  • Also, as their perspectives differ, craftsmen can give each other valuable feedback to improve the quality of their products (CARING).

Now, as we increase the size of the craftsman population, gains increase exponentially. However, the human being is an imperfect one and certain issues can occur:

  • New members of the community might be more interested in taking, rather than giving, contributing nothing in return.
  • Someone might find a way to make profit off the community-shared goods and sell them as his / her own.
  • Out of spite someone may sabotage the joint efforts of the community or identify exploits to misuse the shared goods.

Fortunately, such rotten apples are few and their ill intentions are usually over-shadowed by the good deeds of the community and, despite being loud, quickly forgotten. The gist of it all is that an open-source community will produce things much more efficiently than a company. The flow of information and ideas is less restricted and the community can function as a single, well-coordinated entity out of its own will. The only boundaries are defined by common goals and honest human motivation.
